Mental Health

What is mental health?
Mental health is the state of our emotional, psychological, and social well-being, shaping how we think, feel, and navigate life's ups and downs.
Here are some examples of mental health issues:

Persistent sadness, hopelessness, or disinterest that affects daily life.
Often includes low energy, sleep/appetite changes, and social withdrawal.
Alternates between emotional highs (mania/hypomania) and lows (depression).
Affects energy, decision-making, relationships, and self-image.
Involves intrusive thoughts and repetitive rituals to reduce anxiety.
Can interfere with work, relationships, and personal well-being.
Excessive worry, fear, or panic, either constant or situation-specific.
Includes physical symptoms like rapid heartbeat, sweating, and restlessness.
Develops after trauma, with flashbacks, nightmares, and emotional numbness.
Everyday situations may feel unsafe, disrupting sleep and concentration.
Unhealthy relationship with food, body image, and control.
Can cause serious physical harm and often stem from emotional distress.

Supporting Someone You Care About

Practical guidance for helping others through their mental health journey

Depression

What to Say
  • "I'm here for you, and I care about you"
  • "You're not alone in this - I want to help"
  • "Your feelings are valid, and it's okay to not be okay"
When to Listen
  • When they express feelings without trying to fix them immediately
  • When they share difficult thoughts or suicidal ideation
  • When they're ready to talk about seeking professional help
What NOT to Do
  • Don't say "just think positive" or "snap out of it"
  • Don't compare their struggles to others' experiences
  • Don't take their symptoms personally or make it about you

Bipolar Disorder

What to Say
  • "I notice you're going through a tough time right now"
  • "Your experiences matter, and I want to understand"
  • "Let's work together to find what helps you feel stable"
When to Listen
  • When they want to discuss mood patterns or triggers
  • When they're considering medication or treatment changes
  • When they need to talk about manic or depressive episodes
What NOT to Do
  • Don't try to control or stop manic episodes without professional help
  • Don't dismiss their experiences as "just mood swings"
  • Don't enable destructive behaviors during episodes

OCD

What to Say
  • "I understand this is really difficult for you"
  • "Your feelings about this are completely valid"
  • "Let's find ways to manage this together"
When to Listen
  • When they want to explain their compulsions without judgment
  • When they're struggling with intrusive thoughts
  • When they're ready to discuss treatment options like therapy
What NOT to Do
  • Don't participate in or enable their compulsive behaviors
  • Don't tell them to "just stop" their rituals or obsessions
  • Don't get frustrated or impatient with their need for certainty

Anxiety Disorders

What to Say
  • "You're safe right now, and I'm here with you"
  • "Let's breathe together and take this slowly"
  • "What would help you feel more comfortable right now?"
When to Listen
  • When they're having a panic attack or feeling overwhelmed
  • When they want to share their fears without being judged
  • When they're avoiding situations and need gentle support
What NOT to Do
  • Don't say "just relax" or "calm down" during anxiety
  • Don't force them into anxiety-provoking situations
  • Don't minimize their fears as "irrational" or "silly"

PTSD

What to Say
  • "What happened to you was not your fault"
  • "You're incredibly strong for surviving this"
  • "I believe you, and your feelings are completely valid"
When to Listen
  • When they're having flashbacks or nightmares
  • When they want to share their trauma story at their own pace
  • When they're triggered and need grounding support
What NOT to Do
  • Don't ask for graphic details about their trauma
  • Don't say "you should be over it by now"
  • Don't make sudden movements or loud noises around them

Eating Disorders

What to Say
  • "I care about you as a person, not your appearance"
  • "Your worth isn't determined by your weight or what you eat"
  • "I'm here to support your recovery, whatever that looks like"
When to Listen
  • When they want to talk about their relationship with food
  • When they're struggling with body image or self-worth
  • When they're considering professional treatment or recovery
What NOT to Do
  • Don't comment on their food choices, weight, or appearance
  • Don't try to force them to eat or police their eating
  • Don't use "diet talk" or discuss calories around them
